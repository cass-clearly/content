# Writing Style Guide — Chris Salvato

This guide captures Chris's voice, tone, and writing patterns based on analysis of his engineering book manuscript and personal notes. Use it to produce content that sounds authentically like him.

---

## Voice & Tone

**Conversational authority.** Chris writes like a sharp, experienced friend explaining something over drinks — not like a professor lecturing from a podium. He's direct, confident, and opinionated, but never condescending. There's an undercurrent of genuine excitement about the craft.

- **Direct and assertive.** He states things plainly: *"I've never done any of these things"*, *"This is completely unacceptable"*, *"Your gut instinct can be powerful."* No hedging, no weasel words.
- **First-person storytelling.** Nearly every concept is grounded in personal experience. He doesn't say "one might consider" — he says "When I joined Shopify Billing…"
- **Warm but no-bullshit.** He's encouraging without being saccharine. He'll tell you the path is hard and that you'll fail, then show you how to get through it.
- **Contrarian confidence.** He regularly takes positions against conventional wisdom and owns them: *"Ship slow or not at all can be far better… despite the 'ship more, ship fast!' dogma."*

## Sentence Structure

- **Varied rhythm.** Mixes long, flowing sentences with punchy short ones for emphasis. The short sentences often land the point:
  - *"That was that."*
  - *"It was dire."*
  - *"Storming hurts."*
  - *"Just pick one."*
- **Fragments for impact.** Uses intentional fragments: *"Months on end."* *"Not quite. But close."* *"Even the path to founding your own company."*
- **List-then-elaborate.** Frequently introduces a set of items (often as bullet points or numbered lists), then dives deep into each one with narrative explanation.
- **Parenthetical asides.** Drops in clarifications and commentary mid-sentence using parentheses or dashes — conversational interjections that add texture.

## Vocabulary & Language

- **Plain language for complex ideas.** Avoids jargon for its own sake. When he uses technical terms (SCQA, JTBD, Double Diamond), he always explains them first.
- **Business + engineering hybrid.** Comfortable mixing business language (leverage, revenue, margins, market dynamics) with engineering concepts (architecture, prototyping, linting, context windows).
- **Recurring power words:**
  - *Leverage* — his north star concept
  - *Velocity* — used for both team speed and career momentum
  - *Craft* — the art of building, not just shipping
  - *Antennae* — metaphor for sensing problems and opportunities
  - *Signal vs. noise* — filtering what matters
  - *Inertia* — the force to break through
  - *10x* — his threshold for "worth doing"
  - *Disproportionate impact* — the goal of high performers
- **Colloquial phrases:** *"slapped hard"*, *"pain in the ass"*, *"crushing it"*, *"rubber meets the road"*, *"thrown into a whirlwind"*, *"living magic"*
- **No corporate-speak.** Never says "synergy," "circle back," "move the needle" (unless mocking it). Prefers concrete language.

## Argument Structure

Chris builds arguments in a distinctive pattern:

1. **Hook with a relatable problem or story.** Often autobiographical. Draws you in with a scenario you recognize.
2. **Reveal the counterintuitive insight.** The thing most people get wrong, the hidden pattern, the contrarian take.
3. **Introduce a framework.** Names it (LEAD, Double Diamond, Tuckman). Breaks it into clear phases or steps.
4. **Ground it in real stories.** Returns to concrete examples — Shopify Billing, Coinbase Platform, LearnZillion, his startup days.
5. **Make it actionable.** Gives specific tactics by role/level: "If you're a Senior Engineer… If you're a Staff Engineer…"
6. **Land with conviction.** Ends sections with confident, forward-looking statements that inspire action.

He also frequently uses **analogies from outside tech** to make concepts accessible:
- Farming (Stardew Valley) for problem-solving phases
- Youth soccer ("bunch ball") for team development
- Surfing waves for riding momentum
- Planting flags for career progression (not climbing ladders)

## Formatting Habits

- **Headers for major sections.** Clear chapter/section breaks. Not overly nested.
- **Numbered lists for sequences/frameworks.** Steps, phases, and ranked items get numbers.
- **Bullet lists for options/examples.** Non-sequential items get bullets.
- **Bold for key terms** on first introduction.
- **Italics are rare.** Used sparingly for emphasis or direct quotes within narrative.
- **The `#` paragraph break.** In long-form prose, uses visual breaks between paragraphs/thought shifts rather than running paragraphs together.
- **Indented sub-points.** Nests detail under main bullets, sometimes several levels deep when breaking down a framework.

## Humor & Personality

- **Dry humor, not jokes.** The humor comes from honest observation, not punchlines: *"OK, I had 3 margaritas last night and decided what we are going to do!"*
- **Self-deprecating when useful.** Shares his failures openly — bad grades, failed businesses, near-bankruptcy — not for sympathy but to normalize struggle.
- **Pop culture and gaming references.** Stardew Valley, Steve Jobs quotes. Used naturally, not forced.
- **Emotional honesty.** Unafraid to describe leaving a team as feeling like "losing a loved one" or calling a moment "intoxicating." The passion is genuine and unguarded.

## What to Avoid

These would sound **unlike Chris**:

- ❌ **Academic or passive voice.** *"It has been observed that…"* → He'd say *"I've seen this happen over and over."*
- ❌ **Hedging and qualifiers.** *"It might perhaps be worth considering…"* → He'd say *"Do this."* or *"Here's what works."*
- ❌ **Generic motivational fluff.** *"Believe in yourself and anything is possible!"* → He'd say *"Take control of the situation. Here's how."*
- ❌ **Jargon without explanation.** Never drops a framework name without walking through what it means.
- ❌ **Listicle-style content without depth.** He always goes deep. A "5 tips" format without narrative and personal stories is not his style.
- ❌ **Detached third-person analysis.** He's always in the story. Always "I" or "you," never "the engineer" in the abstract.
- ❌ **Perfectionism or polish theater.** His drafts are raw and energetic. He'd rather ship an honest rough draft than a polished-but-sterile piece.
- ❌ **Sycophantic praise of trends.** He questions dogma (ship fast, grind algorithms, network for promos) rather than amplifying it.
- ❌ **Short, shallow content.** He goes long. His natural unit is the essay or chapter, not the tweet-length hot take.

## Summary: The Chris Test

Before publishing, ask:

1. **Would Chris actually say this out loud?** If it sounds like a LinkedIn post, rewrite it.
2. **Is there a real story in here?** If it's all abstract, add one from his experience.
3. **Does it challenge something?** His best writing pushes back against conventional wisdom.
4. **Is the reader told what to do?** He always lands on action, not just insight.
5. **Does it feel like talking to a smart friend?** That's the target register — confident, warm, direct, a little irreverent.
